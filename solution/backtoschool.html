Solution: Back to School
Answer: ROWAN ATKINSON

Written by eanacra, quatrevingtneuf and chimpaznee

This puzzle consists of many subpuzzles based on popular quiz game shows. Each of them has a single-word answer as a solution. The answers are in turn used to solve the final Only Connect subpuzzle.

Click on a subpuzzle title below to see its solution.

Blockbusters - by quatrevingtneuf

In the show, contestants answer trivia questions in the form “What [letter] [question]” in order to claim a letter space on the board. The answers to the questions are as follows:

Clue Answer
What N might have a special habit? NUN
What R gets counted in a game of baseball? RUN
What P is a common vegetable? PEA
What C is a social deduction board game? COUP
What Q might you catch a ferry at? QUAY
What D is a colour for a horse? DUN
What G is a sort of sandwich? GYRO

Perhaps by saying the words out loud, solvers will notice that the words rhyme with numbers. The letters only appear once each on the given board, so we know to match the answers up directly and place numbers on the board. From here, we proceed as with a game of minesweeper (the number gives how many of the surrounding six hexagons should be marked).


Taking the marked cells in reading order yields the answer to this subpuzzle, SMITH.

Countdown - by quatrevingtneuf

One segment in Countdown involves unscrambling nine letters to form the longest word possible. In this subpuzzle, we are faced with Chinese characters instead. Searching them together, we find that these are all radicals used when typing with the Cangjie input method, one of many ways to type Chinese. To give solvers somewhere to start, a couple radicals are given. Either by trial and error, or by searching a list, we can eventually find the anagram we need. The sequence "月山日女 月山手一月" produces the characters "眼睛", which translate to this subpuzzle's answer, EYE.

Family Feud - by eanacra

In Family Feud, contestants aim to come up with the most popular answer to questions. The break-in to this sub-puzzle is most easily found via the fourth board where OXYGEN and TITANIUM are the only two answers with non-zero score. Indeed, both of these are chemical elements and their scores are equal to the element’s atomic numbers. This is further confirmed by the title "Infinitesimal Amount". From here, we infer that in each board the score of an answer is calculated by a fixed rule clued by the title (by default, the score is 0 if the answer does not satisfy a condition of the rule). We should then pick the answer that scores the highest for each question.

Rule Question Highest-scoring answer Score Letter
N-th prime where N is the highest A1Z26 value present in the answer Name a season. WINTER 83 S
Year in which the film whose last word is the answer won the Academy Award for Best Picture Name a room in Clue. HALL 77 M
N-th triangular number minus one, where N is the answer's length (OEIS sequence A000096) Name a zodiac sign. SAGITTARIUS 65 A
Atomic number of the element Name a metal of antiquity. LEAD 82 R
Sum of A1Z26 values of consonants in the answer Name one of the seven dwarfs. SNEEZY 84 T

The first letters of the titles spell ASCII. Since the highest scores are in the ASCII range for uppercase letters, we can convert them to letters, thus producing the subpuzzle answer: SMART.

Mastermind - by eanacra

We are presented with six questions. The first unusual thing we might notice is the inclusion of the phrase “I’ve started so I’ll finish” in the middle of the last question. This is in fact the catchphrase of the Mastermind presenter Magnus Magnusson who said it whenever the time ran out while he was reading a question on the show. This suggests we should pay attention to the order of the questions.

Next, we turn to the questions themselves but they seem to be very ambiguous. Nevertheless, after listing a few answer candidates for each question, we might notice, for example, the answer SORT works for both the second-last question and the last question. This leads to the gimmick of this subpuzzle: the contestant’s specialist subject is “answering two consecutive questions at the same time”. The questions are given in the order they are asked.

Question Answer 1 Answer 2
What word do you get when the last letter is removed from an alcoholic drink? --- BEE
What word goes in the following blank: "carpenter ___"? BEE ANT
What word do you get when a letter is deleted from a family member? ANT NICE
What word might you use to describe an amiable friend? NICE KIND
What word goes in the following blank: "I couldn't answer the question about what ___ of fish Nemo is"? KIND SORT
What might you - I've started so I'll finish - do to a list of numbers? SORT ---

Reading the first letters of the answers, we obtain the subpuzzle answer: BANKS.

Merv Griffin's Crosswords - by chimpaznee

Instead, each special entry must be transformed in one of two ways before being entered (based on its colour and which half of the grid it is found in). The orange entries must be entered as completions of compound words or phrases, and the blue entries must by entered as rhymes.

Pointless - by eanacra

Pointless is a show where contestants try to come up with answers that the least people thought of. A “Pointless” answer is one that none of the 100 people gave. In this subpuzzle we are given 6 categories and the title “Pointless Things”, so the logical thing to try is to find the “Pointless” answer in each category.

By looking at the list of members for each set, one should spot a commonality between all 6 sets — they each contain something with the thematic substring NIL (this is most easily seen in NIL RECURRING or ONE NIL where the substring is not “hidden”). We can then extract a letter from each entry by going left or right (depending on the arrow’s direction) by the given number, starting at NIL.

Category "Pointless" answer Extracted letter
Films directed by Sean Ellis (←7) METRO MANILA M
Players for the Atlanta Hawks in the 2021-22 season (←1) DANILO GALLINARI A
EPs by Porcupine Tree (→5) NIL RECURRING R
Films directed by Cameron Crowe (→4) VANILLA SKY K
Neil Finn albums (←1) ONE NIL E
Agatha Christie books featuring Poirot (←7) DEATH ON THE NILE T

This gives us the subpuzzle answer, MARKET.

The Price is Right - by quatrevingtneuf

Each of the items has been assigned a "price", but these certainly aren't things you would normally buy. All of the words are 3 letters long, which helps with identification:

CAT

Reading the first letters gives us the clue CHAMPERNOWNE. Champernowne's constant can be generated by listing all the numbers in a given base in order (following a decimal point). If we try the same thing with letters (i.e. ABC...XYZAAABAC...ZXZYZZAAAAAB...), the first occurrence of each of the words above is at the index given by its "price". Notice that if had missed the CHAMPERNOWNE clue, we may have been able to surmise this rule from a couple patterns (the small gap between CAT and HAT, "price" in a similar order of magnitude for POP/EVE and MAN/WAX, higher "prices" for words where all the letters appear later in the alphabet, etc.).

Now all that's left to do is to find the word that appears at the index with the question mark underneath. It's probably easiest to calculate this or write a simple script for it (note that sheets formulae won't easily be able generate the string because it's too long). At that position, you'll find the answer, NAVY.

University Challenge - by eanacra

We are presented with 6 images each accompanied by a number after a double-headed arrow. One can reverse image search these images easily, but as expected indexing into the names immediately doesn’t result in a word. Having a basic understanding of how the show works is helpful here — as explained in the show’s Wikipedia article, teams consist of 4 members and represent a university, except the colleges of the University of Cambridge and the University of Oxford are allowed to enter independent teams. Even without this knowledge, a search of “university” with some key words from the titles of the artwork will eventually lead us to the realization that each image clues a Cambridge or Oxford college.

What does the double-headed arrow represent? With this new discovery, we might guess that each Cambridge college is connected in some way with an Oxford college. Indeed, clued by the instruction "Identify the sibling in each case", we find that every college has a sister college in the other university. (Be careful not to confuse Oxford’s St Catherine’s College with Cambridge’s St Catharine’s College.)

Image College Sister college Index Letter

Indexing the given numbers into the sister colleges, we obtain the subpuzzle answer, STRING.

Wheel of Fortune - by quatrevingtneuf

If we simply try to fill in the blanks as normal, we may not find satisfying phrases for a couple of these grids. The gimmick here is that we need to spin the whole board around, flipping all the blanks and letters upside down. Letters that are invariant under 180° rotation are given, along with Ws that become Ms when rotated. The completed phrases are:

  • FRESH PRINCE OF BEL-AIR
  • BICYCLE KICK
  • INKAY EVOLVING INTO MALAMAR
  • BUNDT CAKE

All of these things involve flipping upside down. Taking the letters indicated by blue boxes, we get our subpuzzle answer: BELT.

Who Wants to Be a Millionaire? - by eanacra

We are presented with 4 multiple-choice questions in the style of the show along with a bar chart and an image showing three lifelines. We can quickly notice that each question contains a number and 3 of these numbers do not make sense. We should use the lifelines! First, apply the “50:50” lifeline to half the numbers in each question (they are all even). Now that the questions make sense, we can proceed to answer them. It turns out all the answers are exactly 5 letters long, equal to the number of digits in each option. This is where the second lifeline “Phone a Friend” comes into play — we can convert each letter to a number via the mapping on a telephone keypad (e.g. D/E/F equals 3) and one of the options is the correct answer. Now observe that the 4 correct answers come from distinct options so we should sort them by the option letter (the questions are presented in alphabetical order).

How do we extract the answer now? By invoking the final lifeline “Ask the Audience”. The lifeline yielded the percentages 20%, 20%, 40%, 20% respectively and since 20% of 5 is 1, we extract the first letters of the answers corresponding to options A, B, D and the first two letters of the answer corresponding to option C.

Question Answer Option
A song from which Disney film was nominated for Best Original Song at the 178 89th Academy Awards? MOANA A
Fill in the blank: Haydn's Symphony No. 202 101 is commonly known as "The ___". CLOCK D
What is the 8 4th month of the year? APRIL B
What is the adjective in the title of the Roald Dahl novel published 122 61 years ago? GIANT C
Option Answer Percentage Extraction
A MOANA 20 M
B APRIL 20 A
C GIANT 40 GI
D CLOCK 20 C

Concatenating these letters gives the subpuzzle answer, MAGIC.

Only Connect - by quatrevingtneuf and eanacra

At long last, we arrive at the end: a connecting wall. Our subpuzzle answers can be sorted into four groups of four. These four groups of four reference films and TV shows, as clued by the movie camera emojis.

Category Subpuzzle Answers Film

In true Only Connect fashion, we find the final link between these four films/shows: they all prominently feature ROWAN ATKINSON, the final answer to this puzzle.

Author’s Notes

By coincidence, this puzzle has the structure of the main round — 4 groups of 4 answers contributing to a meta! The initial idea for this puzzle was completely different but was scrapped as it significantly overlapped with a past MIT Mystery Hunt puzzle. We placed this puzzle at the first half of the main round to encourage more teams to try it because we know it will take a long time due to the large number of subpuzzles. Most subpuzzles underwent revision after testsolving in an effort to make them easier and this was the last puzzle in the seasons round to pass testsolving.

We aimed to make the “bean” puzzles harder on average compared to the other 3 sets since it was a lot easier to get the answer with MR BEAN. We considered whether we should replace MR BEAN with a less obvious film, provide enumerations or the movie camera emoji for Only Connect, but ultimately we decided to be as generous as possible because the puzzle was already quite long. However, the presence of enumerations has resulted in testsolvers short-circuiting the connecting wall (with a hyphenated 6-3 being very specific), so we tried to test it without them. Fortunately, we managed to get a successful test of the wall in this state.

Blockbusters: It seems that many people got caught up on the pronunciation of QUAY, which incidentally also appears in a homophone clue in another puzzle in the hunt.

The Chase: The third question used to be "What is commonly associated with Squidward?" with the answer being WRATH (a sin). This tripped up testsolvers, so (un)fortunately, the puzzle is now free of any SpongeBob SquarePants fan theories!

Countdown: This idea came to me while playing JNDLE. I don't type with this method, but I do find it interesting. Originally, I didn't want to provide any givens, but this was ultimately deemed too cruel for 1/16th of a puzzle.

Family Feud: To my surprise, most testsolvers were completely stuck on this subpuzzle despite the fairly standard atomic number in board 4. As a result, I added the titles to simplify the subpuzzle and clue the extraction.

Jeopardy!: I love luge.

Mastermind: This entire puzzle was partly inspired by thinking about writing a puzzle in the style of this subpuzzle. Originally the questions were not sorted in the extraction order and the second-last question was harder, but this was changed after no testsolvers solved this subpuzzle.

Pointless: I’m not entirely satisfied with the current puzzle as it doesn’t fully utilize the interesting Pointless format.

Taskmaster: This was inspired by the studio task in Series 5 Episode 4 which involved a game of “Greg says, Alex says”.

Tenable: I had to rewrite this subpuzzle after Boris Johnson resigned because one of the lists was “The 10 most recent British prime ministers”.

Who Wants to be a Millionaire: This is my favourite sub-puzzle out of the 7 that I wrote, because it uses elements of the show very well and is still very accessible at the same time.
