Solution: Journal Overtakeages
Answer: PAIR OF BINOCULARS

Written by chuttiekang and chimpaznee

Each journal entry has two italicized parts. These can be solved as crossword-style clues. Though some of them are ambiguous at first, we should soon realize that the answers to the clues have a special property: each pair of words actually contains a pair of synonyms. For instance, the first pair of clues resolves to DIRE and DANGER. This is hinted at by the puzzle title (Overtakeages/Passages). The letters that aren't part of the synonym pair are identical across both words, so we should take note of them. Furthermore, the “dates” serve as enumerations to the clue answers.

The letters don't seem to spell out anything meaningful yet! But seeing that the journal entries are all alphabetical, we should find a way to sort them.

In this new ordering of the letters, we can read out “PWIND OF CASEOCULARS”. We can use the synonym substitution mechanism to convert “WIND” to “AIR” and “CASE” to “BIN” to make valid words. In doing so, we arrive at the final answer, a PAIR OF BINOCULARS, which is an item commonly carried while travelling around the world.

Author’s Notes

chimpaznee: This puzzle gave us a real challenge when it came to theming it. The sorting mechanism went through a large number of versions, including using national flowers, and we went through at least 2 different country maps before arriving at the current one. In hindsight, I think a more compelling order would have been to put the synonym pairs in 2 different journal entries and have them form a cycle, but I think that it would have been more difficult to both discover the pairs and parse the final cluephrase, and it would also require rewriting all entries.

One thing I'm happy with, though, is the decision to italicize the clue bits, which allowed us to have some creative liberty with the entries.

During the hunt, this puzzle tripped up a much large number of teams than we expected. There were two main sticking points: The fact that the letters need to be put on the map, and the final cluephrase being very hard to parse. Unfortunately, the glaring problems weren't caught in testsolving. If your team got confused by this puzzle - sorry!

In hindsight, we should have definitely worked harder to make the puzzle more elegant, as it seemed to have separate ideas crammed together in an inelegant way (hidden synonyms, and country anagrams).
